The Dark Matter Mystery

You're looking at a spiral galaxy with 20,000 stars orbiting a supermassive black hole.

The Problem

According to Newton's gravity and the visible mass, stars far from the center should orbit slowly.

But when we measure real galaxies, outer stars orbit way too fast.

This isn't a small error. The outer stars move 2-3x faster than they "should." This has been confirmed in thousands of galaxies over 50 years of observations.

The Galactic Center

What's at the middle? A supermassive black hole (like Sagittarius A* in our Milky Way) surrounded by 8,000 densely packed old stars. The orange glow is the accretion disk — matter spiraling in.

What Does This Mean?

Three possibilities:

1. There's invisible "dark matter" holding galaxies together (~85% of all matter)
2. Our theory of gravity is wrong at galactic scales (MOND)
3. Something we haven't thought of yet
Nobody knows the answer. This is one of the biggest unsolved problems in physics. Real scientists are working on this right now. You're looking at actual scientific uncertainty.

The Data is Rock Solid

Vera Rubin measured this in the 1970s. It's been confirmed thousands of times since. The mystery isn't the measurement — it's the explanation.
